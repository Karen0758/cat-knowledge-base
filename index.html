Care Journey

Instead of a generic age timeline, this section is organized around the care responsibilities a cat parent takes on over time.

Before adoption

Make the home manageable first

  • Prepare a safe room, litter box, water station, scratching area, hiding place, and resting spots.
  • Plan the first exam, vaccines, parasite prevention, spay/neuter timing, and emergency clinic.
  • For multi-cat homes, read the new-cat introduction guidance before any direct meeting.
0-1 year

Kittens need immunity, growth support, and social learning

  • Kittens are more infection-prone; the vaccine series and 6-month booster matter.
  • Use kitten/growth-stage complete diets and transition foods over 7-10 days.
  • Start carrier training, tooth brushing, nail trims, and calm feeding routines early.
1-6 years

Young adults need durable routines

  • Schedule at least annual exams and record weight, body condition, and muscle condition.
  • Use small meals, puzzle feeding, and measured portions to prevent obesity.
  • Treat oral changes, urination changes, and behavior shifts as health signals.
7-10 years

Mature adults need earlier screening

  • Watch kidneys, blood pressure, dental disease, urinary signs, weight, and chronic pain.
  • Litter boxes should be large enough and easy to enter for painful or aging cats.
  • Discuss monitoring plans with a veterinarian before long-term medications.
10+ years

Seniors need comfort and trend management

  • Senior guidelines recommend more frequent reassessment, often at least every 6 months.
  • Look beyond body weight: muscle loss, pain, frailty, and cognition also matter.
  • Night vocalization, house-soiling, and reduced activity may reflect disease or pain.
Any age

Uncertainty and emergencies need a veterinarian

  • Urinary blockage, breathing trouble, seizures, poisoning, persistent vomiting, trauma, or not eating cannot wait.
  • FIP, stones, and chronic pain cannot be judged from one symptom alone.
  • The handbook helps you ask better questions; it does not replace diagnosis.

New Parent Q&A

Feeding

Q1: What is the first thing people miss when choosing food?

It is not “grain-free” or “high protein.” First confirm that the food is complete and balanced for the cat's current stage. The guidelines recommend diets with a nutritional adequacy statement and caution against using treats, supplements, or non-sterilized raw foods as the sole diet.

Nutrition section

Q2: Why is leaving one bowl out all day not ideal?

Cats naturally tend to eat small meals, frequently, and alone. The AAFP feeding statement notes that one or two large meals, or unlimited easy food access, can reduce foraging behavior and contribute to boredom, stress, and overeating.

Feeding programs

Q3: Are treats allowed?

Yes, but the feeding guidelines give a useful limit: treats should provide no more than 10% of daily calories. More than that can unbalance the diet and interfere with weight loss or therapeutic urinary diets.

Feeding programs

Q4: Why should food transitions be gradual?

The life-stage guidelines recommend transitioning diets over 7-10 days. This gives you time to watch for soft stool, vomiting, refusal to eat, and stress. Kittens, sensitive cats, and therapeutic diets especially should not be switched abruptly.

Care stage guidelines

Home & Behavior

Q5: How big should a litter box be?

A specific but often missed guideline: the box should be at least 1.5 times the cat's length from nose to tail tip. Many commercial boxes are too small, especially for mature, senior, painful, or urinary-problem cats.

Elimination section

Q6: What do multi-cat homes commonly misread?

They assume there is no conflict because there is no fighting. The 2024 intercat tension guidelines highlight subtle signs such as staring, blocking, guarding resources, avoiding, and freezing. Some house-soiling happens because one cat blocks another cat's route to the litter box.

Intercat tension

Q7: If a cat seems lonely, should I get another cat?

Not automatically. The intercat tension guidelines list “cats get lonely and need a companion” as a common myth. A second cat may help or may create chronic stress; individual temperament, resource distribution, and gradual introduction matter.

Intercat tension

Q8: If two cats play roughly, should I separate them immediately?

Not always. The intercat tension guidelines separate play from conflict: role-switching and brief chasing may be play; one-way pursuit, freezing, hiding, hissing, blocking, or tufts of fur suggest tension and need intervention.

Relationship signs

Medical Prevention

Q9: Why shouldn't care plans be based on age alone?

AAHA/AAFP groups cats into kitten, young adult, mature adult, senior, and end-of-life stages, but age is only a starting point. Each visit should reassess body condition, muscle condition, behavior, disease risks, and the home environment.

Care stage guidelines

Q10: Are vaccines just a yearly full set?

No. Vaccination guidelines separate core and non-core vaccines; non-core vaccines depend on exposure risk. A vaccine visit should also include a physical exam, risk assessment, and caregiver education.

Vaccination

Q11: What do caregivers often do wrong at the vet?

Cat friendly interaction guidance centers on perceived control. Avoid forcing the cat out of the carrier, staring into the cat's face, holding the cat tightly to comfort them, or making shushing sounds that may resemble hissing.

Cat friendly visits

Q12: Does a normal appetite mean the mouth is healthy?

No. The 2025 oral health guidelines specifically warn that appetite changes are uncommon in cats with oral and dental disease. Drooling, one-sided chewing, bad breath, face sensitivity, or reduced activity deserve evaluation.

Oral health

Mature & Senior Care

Q13: Why isn't senior care just “eat less and move less”?

Senior care is trend-based: weight, muscle, blood pressure, pain, cognition, chronic disease, and quality of life. Some senior cats need more calories to maintain condition, so mechanical food restriction can be harmful.

Senior care

Q14: Is night vocalization in an older cat just clinginess?

Do not assume that. The life-stage guidelines note that night vocalization may reflect hearing or vision decline, cognitive dysfunction, pain, hyperthyroidism, or hypertension, so it deserves medical context.

Senior behavior

Q15: Can I buy pain medicine for my cat myself?

No. NSAID guidance stresses that cats have unique metabolism, and chronic pain often overlaps with kidney disease or other comorbidities. Long-term pain control needs prescription choice, screening, efficacy checks, and adverse-effect monitoring.

NSAIDs

Q16: Why track muscle, not just body weight, in older cats?

The life-stage guidelines recommend recording weight, body condition, and muscle condition throughout life. Older cats can lose muscle even when weight changes slowly; photos and serial records help reveal subtle trends.

Senior assessment

Disease Risk

Q17: Do urinary stones always need surgery?

Not always. The urolithiasis statement notes that struvite bladder stones are often dissolvable with the right therapeutic diet, while calcium oxalate stones are not. Stone type and location require imaging, urinalysis, and veterinary assessment.

Urolithiasis

Q18: Why can't urinary therapeutic diets be mixed freely?

The urolithiasis statement notes that dissolution diets have specific composition goals and should be fed as 100% of the diet or only with compatible treats. Mixing foods can undermine urine pH and mineral-control targets.

Therapeutic diet

Q19: Can one test diagnose FIP?

The FIP guidelines emphasize building suspicion “brick by brick”: history, age, source, physical findings, effusion, imaging, laboratory data, and viral testing all have to fit together. No single result replaces the full diagnostic picture.

FIP diagnosis

Q20: Can a household with immunocompromised people have a cat?

Usually the answer is not a simple yes or no; it is risk management. The zoonoses guidelines recommend collaboration among veterinarians, caregivers, and physicians, using preventive care, parasite control, hygiene, and avoidance of high-risk foods to reduce transmission risk.

Zoonoses
